## Bottom line

Your best ICP is **not simply “software companies with no AI.”** It is:

**Pre-2025 incumbent software companies with proprietary workflow/data assets, enough customers to feel competitive pressure, and either no public AI narrative or a shallow AI feature that does not yet change the customer’s core workflow.**

That is why **Mojio** and **Automate** are strong examples. Mojio has connected-mobility software, large-scale driving data, and partner/customer logos; its own press room says it was founded in 2012 and has real-world data from 9B+ miles across 1M+ vehicles. ([Mojio](https://www.moj.io/press-room/)) Automate says it has 40+ years of dealership-management software experience and is trusted by 1,000+ dealerships. ([Automate](https://www.automate.co.za/)) In both cases, I did not find obvious homepage-level “AI” positioning in the parsed public pages I checked, which is exactly the “valuable data, weak AI narrative” pattern. ([Mojio](https://www.moj.io/))

Hootsuite is a different kind of good target: it has scale, workflows, social/listening data, and public AI messaging. Its site says it has been around 18 years and has had 25M users, while its AI pages emphasize captions, post ideas, hashtags, images, and OwlyGPT using real-time social feeds. ([Hootsuite](https://www.hootsuite.com/)) That makes Hootsuite more of a **“help us make AI strategic, not just a feature”** target than a **“we have no AI”** target.

Geotab is more borderline. Its Ace page describes an AI assistant built into MyGeotab, natural-language fleet-data answers, predictive models, benchmarking, secure/read-only access, and Google Vertex AI integration. ([Geotab](https://www.geotab.com/products/ace-ai-assistant/)) That could still be “assistant over existing analytics,” but it is not an obviously empty AI wrapper from public evidence alone. I would qualify Geotab-type companies only if your wedge is **workflow automation, agentic actions, adoption, or monetization beyond a read-only assistant.**

## The qualification process I would use

### 1\. Hard gates

A company should pass all of these before you spend human research time:

| Gate | Pass condition |
| :---- | :---- |
| Founded before 2025 | Verified via LinkedIn, Crunchbase, PitchBook, company site, press releases, or registries |
| Product company | Sells repeatable software/platform, not primarily services/consulting |
| B2B or B2B2C | Has business customers, enterprise accounts, or channel/partner distribution |
| Has proprietary workflow/data | Customer operational data, transaction history, documents, messages, trips, claims, inventory, tickets, inspections, schedules, diagnostics, etc. |
| Enough budget | Roughly 25–2,000 employees, or smaller only if clearly funded/high-ARR |
| Not AI-native | AI is not the core founding premise, category, or main differentiator |

The key is the **data/workflow gate**. A 20-year-old SaaS company with no unique data is not attractive. A 20-year-old vertical platform with messy domain data and manual customer workflows is highly attractive.

---

### 2\. Score “AI posture” separately from “AI potential”

Use two separate scores so you do not reject companies just because they mention AI.

| Score | AI posture | What it means |
| ----: | :---- | :---- |
| 0 | No visible AI | Great if the company has strong data/workflows |
| 1 | Generic AI copy | “AI-powered,” “smarter,” “save time,” but no feature depth |
| 2 | Thin feature | Writer, summarizer, chatbot, plain-language search, dashboard Q\&A |
| 3 | Grounded assistant | Uses product/customer data, but mostly read-only or advisory |
| 4 | Embedded AI workflow | AI changes user workflow, has docs, permissions, pricing, case studies |
| 5 | AI-native / agentic | AI is central to product, GTM, pricing, and differentiation |

Your sweet spot is usually **0–2**. Score **3** can be good if you sell deeper AI transformation. Score **4–5** is usually disqualified unless you are selling infrastructure, evaluations, observability, governance, or partnerships.

---

### 3\. Score the company on a 100-point lead rubric

| Dimension | Weight | Strong signal |
| :---- | ----: | :---- |
| AI gap | 30 | No AI, or one generic AI feature with no public adoption proof |
| Data/workflow moat | 25 | Proprietary operational data inside recurring customer workflows |
| Commercial urgency | 20 | Competitors launching AI, customers asking for AI, board/PE pressure, churn risk |
| Budget/access | 15 | 25–2,000 employees, funded, profitable, enterprise customers, active hiring |
| Feasibility | 10 | APIs, integrations, cloud product, docs, clear permissions model |

**Tier A:** 75–100 **Tier B:** 60–74 **Reject/nurture:** below 60

**Vertical focus is part of the data/workflow moat, not a separate line.** This
dimension rewards *depth in one industry*, not breadth across many. A company that
describes itself as serving everyone (“for businesses of all sizes,” “across
industries”) is read as horizontal and capped on this dimension; a focused
vertical incumbent earns full credit plus a specialization bonus. That is why a
broad horizontal platform should not outrank a niche vertical incumbent that shows
the same raw data signals — the ICP is vertical software incumbents, not broad SaaS.

Mojio-like and Automate-like companies are Tier A because they appear data-rich, vertical, mature, and under-positioned on AI. Hootsuite-like companies can be Tier A or B depending on whether your offer is “build the first AI feature” or “make AI core to product strategy.”

## What counts as “limited public AI traction”

Do not rely on whether the site says “AI.” Look for evidence of substance.

A company has **limited AI traction** if most of these are missing:

| Evidence type | Strong AI traction if present |
| :---- | :---- |
| Product docs | AI-specific docs, admin controls, permissions, evaluation behavior, data boundaries |
| Pricing | Paid AI SKU, add-on, usage-based pricing, entitlement management |
| Case studies | Customer stories specifically about AI outcomes |
| Adoption proof | Usage numbers, named customers, ROI, benchmark claims |
| Release cadence | Multiple AI product updates over 6–12 months |
| Reviews | G2/Capterra/App Store users mention AI positively |
| Workflow depth | AI can trigger actions, not just summarize or draft |
| Data grounding | Clear explanation of how it uses product/customer/domain data |
| Governance | Security, audit, privacy, hallucination handling, human approval paths |

The “board said we had to” profile is usually: **big AI headline, one page, generic feature, no adoption proof, no docs, no pricing, no workflow ownership.**

## Search approach: use list-first, not keyword-first

The biggest trap is searching for “AI software companies.” That mostly finds companies that are already talking about AI. Your best targets may be invisible to AI searches.

Use this sequence instead:

### Step 1: Build the software-company universe

Start from software directories and databases, then enrich:

* G2, Capterra, Software Advice, GetApp, SourceForge, Product Hunt  
* Crunchbase, PitchBook, Dealroom, Tracxn, Apollo, LinkedIn  
* BuiltWith for SaaS/pricing-page/web-tech signals  
* Vertical directories: dealership software, fleet software, construction software, field-service software, insurance software, legal practice software, property-management software, healthcare admin software, etc.

For market scale, G2 says its 2026 global software-company list is drawn from **116,198 total vendors in that category**, and G2’s 2026 awards article says there are **175,000+ vendors listed on G2** overall. ([G2](https://www.g2.com/best-software-companies/top-companies)) BuiltWith separately lists **64,800 websites with “SaaS” on the homepage**, which is useful but undercounts vertical software companies that do not call themselves SaaS. ([BuiltWith](https://builtwith.com/website-lists/Saas))

### Step 2: Filter to high-probability categories

Prioritize verticals where companies have domain data but may not have AI-native teams:

| Highest-priority verticals | Why they fit |
| :---- | :---- |
| Automotive, DMS, fleet, telematics | Matches Mojio/Automate pattern; lots of operational data |
| Field service, dispatch, maintenance | Scheduling, routing, work orders, technician notes |
| Logistics, TMS, WMS, last-mile | Optimization, exceptions, ETA, capacity, documents |
| Construction, property, facilities | Old workflows, documents, estimates, compliance |
| Insurance, claims, risk | Documents, decisions, workflows, regulatory pressure |
| Healthcare admin, RCM, patient ops | Complex workflows, high labor cost, data constraints |
| Manufacturing, ERP, MES, CMMS | Maintenance, quality, inventory, production exceptions |
| GovTech, compliance, permitting | Document-heavy workflows, older incumbents |
| Education/admin/LMS | Large user bases, content/workflow opportunities |
| Legal/accounting practice software | Document-heavy, but watch for crowded AI-native entrants |

Horizontal marketing, sales, support, and BI tools can still work, but they are more crowded and more likely to have already bolted on generic AI.

### Step 3: Crawl each company for AI evidence

For every company/domain, search:

```
site:{domain} AI OR "artificial intelligence" OR "copilot" OR "GPT" OR "assistant" OR "machine learning" OR "generative"
site:{domain}/blog AI OR "copilot" OR "ChatGPT"
site:{domain}/docs AI OR "assistant" OR "permissions" OR "model"
site:{domain}/changelog AI OR "copilot" OR "assistant"
site:{domain}/pricing AI OR "add-on" OR "usage"
```

Then classify the result into:

```
0 = no AI
1 = generic mention
2 = thin AI feature
3 = grounded assistant
4 = embedded workflow AI
5 = AI-native
```

### Step 4: Use “generic AI wrapper” searches to find board-pressure targets

These searches find companies that are trying to show AI momentum but may not have deep AI substance:

```
"AI assistant built into" "software"
"ask questions in plain language" "AI assistant" "dashboard"
"turn data into instant answers" "AI assistant"
"AI-powered" "save time" "request a demo" "software"
"generate captions" "AI writer" "platform"
"generate posts" "AI writer" "social media management"
"powered by ChatGPT" "platform" "software"
"powered by OpenAI" "request a demo" "software"
"introduces AI assistant" "customers" "software"
"launches AI assistant" "SaaS" "no code"
```

Then manually inspect whether the AI feature has docs, pricing, named customers, and workflow depth. If it does not, it may be a strong “board said we had to” target.

### Step 5: Use “negative-space” searches for excellent targets

These are better for Mojio/Automate-style companies:

```
"dealership management system" "software" "40 years"
"fleet management software" "API" "telematics" "platform"
"connected mobility platform" "SaaS" "data"
"field service management software" "founded" "customers"
"claims management software" "workflow" "API"
"construction management software" "document management" "founded"
"maintenance management software" "work orders" "integrations"
"property management software" "resident portal" "API"
```

For each result, run the AI crawl. The highest-value targets will often have **zero AI search hits** but strong data/workflow language.

## TAM estimate

Because I do not know your ACV, I would estimate TAM in **account count first**, then convert to dollar TAM.

### Market context

The software universe is large enough to support this ICP. Gartner forecasts worldwide software spending of about **$1.43T in 2026**, growing 14.7%. ([Gartner](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-02-03-gartner-forecasts-worldwide-it-spending-to-grow-10-point-8-percent-in-2026-totaling-6-point-15-trillion-dollars)) Enterprise genAI spend is also moving quickly: Menlo Ventures estimates companies spent **$37B on generative AI in 2025**, with **$19B** going to user-facing products/software using underlying AI models. ([Menlo Ventures](https://menlovc.com/perspective/2025-the-state-of-generative-ai-in-the-enterprise/)) G2 also notes generative AI features have been added across nearly **200 software categories**, which supports the idea that AI pressure is broad across software, not limited to a few categories. ([Learn Hub](https://learn.g2.com/generative-ai-in-saas))

### Account TAM model

I would use three universe anchors:

| Case | Starting universe | Source basis |
| :---- | ----: | :---- |
| Conservative | 64,800 | SaaS-keyword websites from BuiltWith |
| Base | 116,198 | G2 global software-company category vendor count |
| Aggressive | 175,000 | Total vendors listed on G2 |

Then apply these assumptions:

| Filter | Assumption | Rationale |
| :---- | ----: | :---- |
| Founded pre-2025 / active | 90% | Most current software companies were founded before 2025 |
| B2B or workflow/data-rich | 60% | Excludes consumer apps, simple tools, services, low-data products |
| Limited or performative AI | 55% | The least certain assumption; should be validated with sampling |
| Serviceable budget/reach | 45% | English-speaking/North America-Europe-friendly, enough budget, reachable decision-makers |

### Result

| Case | Broad account TAM | Serviceable account market |
| :---- | ----: | ----: |
| Conservative | \~19,000 companies | \~8,700 companies |
| Base | \~34,500 companies | \~15,500 companies |
| Aggressive | \~52,000 companies | \~23,400 companies |

My working estimate: **20k–55k global account TAM**, with a more actionable **8k–23k serviceable account market** once you filter for budget, geography, reachability, and urgency.

### Dollar TAM by ACV

| ACV | Broad TAM | Serviceable market |
| ----: | ----: | ----: |
| $25k/year | \~$0.5B–$1.3B | \~$0.2B–$0.6B |
| $75k/year | \~$1.4B–$3.9B | \~$0.7B–$1.8B |
| $150k/year | \~$2.9B–$7.8B | \~$1.3B–$3.5B |

If this is a consulting/project motion rather than annual software, replace ACV with **first-year project value** and then model expansion separately.

## Recommended initial GTM wedge

Start with **vertical software incumbents**, not broad SaaS.

Best first campaigns:

1. **Automotive software:** DMS, telematics, fleet, service-shop, warranty, parts, dealer CRM.  
2. **Field-service and maintenance software:** dispatch, inspections, work orders, asset history.  
3. **Logistics and operations software:** TMS, WMS, routing, delivery, compliance docs.  
4. **Construction/property/facilities software:** projects, documents, assets, tickets, estimates.  
5. **Insurance/claims/compliance software:** documents, decisions, workflow queues, audit trails.

The outreach angle should be:

“You already own the domain data and workflow. Your competitors are turning AI into a product narrative. We help turn your existing data into defensible AI features that customers will actually use, not just a chatbot or writer.”

That positioning fits the ICP much better than “we build AI features.”
